Exploratory Data Analysis



In the world of data science, it is quite easy to get lost in the sea of Kaggle Notebooks and quick code snippets. However, as I grow in my craft, I've realized that data analytics is not just a set of tools and techniques - it is a philosophy of understanding, and a search for truth.

I believe a complete data project requires a "Full-Stack" approach: deep reading, critical thinking, exhaustive exploration, and artistic communication. This post outlines my personal framwork for data analytics, using the classic UCI Wine Quality Dataset as a case study.

01 / The Art of 'Slow' Data Entry

Before writing a single line of Python code, I start with manual immersion into the project and the data. While most jump straight into df.describe() and df.info(), I prefer and recommend to:

  • Read the Research: This datasets stems from the Minho region of Portugal, originally published in 2009, representing "Vinho Verde" wines. I study the research paper to understand the thoughts of the researchers.
  • Excel Scroll: I open the CSV and scroll to the very last row. I want to see the headers and the raw formating of the dataset. It also helps me get a feel of what the data looks like.
  • Understanding the Target: I try to understand the target variable and what it means. A crucial relaisation here is that the "Quality" is not an objective chemical truth - it is a measure of human preference. It is the median score of at least three blind tasters. Understanding this shifts the project from "measuring wine" to "predicting human preferred taste".

02 / Themematic Exploration

To avoid aimless exploration, I establish themes with the view of What questions can this dataset answer?. I combine my own curiosity with the power of AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini) to brainstorm angles that a business manager would actually care about.

  • Feature-driven Exploration: Selecting groups of variables and examining how they relate to the target variable.
    Example: Chemical Fingerprint: What markers are non-negotiables for premium vs. budget wines?
  • Question-driven Exploration: Starting with an abstract question and using the data to investigate it.
  • AI-assisted Ideation: I use tools like ChatGPT, Cladue, and Gemini by feeding them the dataset description and column details. They generate exploration themes, which I then implement myself. This is one of my favorite approaches to be honest. It expands my thinking, mirrors real-world stakeholder questions and helps sharpen my skill.

04 / Predictive Analytics

My approach to Machine Learning is 'no stone left unturned'. I treat most projetc as a dual challenge:

  • Regression: I predicting the exact score (0–10) using models like Random Forest, XGBoost, and SVR..
  • Classification: Categorizing wine into Low (0-4), Medium (5-7), and High (8-10) quality using Logistic Regression, Naive Bayes, and Neural Networks.
  • Rigorous Evaluation: I compare mI compare models using RMSE/MAE for regression and F1-Score/ROC-AUC for classification, followed by hyperparameter tuning to ensure the best possible performance.

05 / Automation and Scalability

To mimic a real-world production environment, I don't stop at a notebook. I write a Python script to automate the entire pipeline. This ensures that if a wine producer provides a continuous stream of new data, the system can automatically preprocess it and output a quality prediction using the champion model. Please don't stop at Jupyter Notebooks, I beg you.

06 / Visualization

It is important to create visualisations using Python libraries such as seaborn. But, beyond that, I design business-oriented dashboards: I sketch ideas before building them. I focus on how insights are communicated, not just displayed.

My perspective on visualisation was shaped by my experience with Viz for Social Good (VFSG). While I initially struggled with confidence and did not publish my work, I learned a lot by observing professionals—their design thinking, storytelling, and execution. I also draw inspiration from sources like Tableau Public and The Economist.

For me, visualisation is an art. Communicating insights effectively is just as important as the analysis itself.

07 / Compelling Reporting

The final step is the one most often skipped: The Compelling Report. I believe in documenting everything—the successes, the failed models, and the "why" behind the results. A great document respects the work done; it translates the technical complexity into a story that a business owner can use to optimize their cultivation and maximize their revenue. I think this is an act of respect for the craft of data science, and for all who intend to read the work.
